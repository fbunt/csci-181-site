The Motherboard Manual: The Build Bible

Once you have all of your components and are ready to build, take out the motherboard and read the manual. All of it. They are not very long but they contain almost all of the technical information you will need for the build. Having trouble finding a header? Check the manual. Don't know which RAM slots to use? Check the manual. Not sure how to install the CPU? Check the manual. It's all there. Also, once you start plugging things into the IO headers and panel, the diagrams are life savers.

Minimize Static

Static is one of your worst enemies when building a PC so minimizing the chances of a static discharge goes a long way. You don't want to accidentally kill a component before it's even plugged into the motherboard. The best bet for preventing static discharges is to use an anti-static wristband. If one isn't available, you just have to be smart. Don't wear socks or slippers while on carpet, don't build on a conductive surface and always touch a conductive surface before handling a component. When I do a build at home, I will go barefoot just to make sure.

Always Do a Bench Build First

A very common mistake for new builders is to put everything in the case before testing the system. It is almost a guarantee that something won't be quite right and you'll have to pull it all out again. This is a huge pain so a new builder should always do a bench build first and get the system to POST before installing everything in the case. To do this, set the motherboard on its box and begin plugging everything into it. The boxes are quite sturdy, non-conductive, and just the right size. Once everything is installed on the board and properly connected to the power supply, power it on (don't forget to flip the switch on the power supply, too). You can find how to turn the motherboard on in its manual. If it POSTs then all is swell and you can tear it down an put everything in the case. If not, then you have everything right in front of you, ready for troubleshooting.

Motherboard IO Shield and Case Standoffs First

Two other very common mistakes are forgetting to put the motherboard's IO shield in the case and installing motherboard without the case's standoff screws in place. The first just means that you have to pull everything out again while the second mistake is a sure fire way to fry your board and potentially everything plugged into it. When the standoffs are not in place, the board will make contact with the back of the case and cause a short. Thus, the first thing I do when I take the case out of its packaging is to install the standoffs followed by the IO shield. One thing to keep in mind when installing the IO shield is that the cut edges on it are incredibly sharp so treat it with respect when handling.

Installing RAM: Push Harder

When installing sticks of RAM on the motherboard, the answer to almost every problem is just push harder. RAM kits are notorious for requiring excessive amounts of force to insert. On one of the building forums that I frequent, there is a minimum of one post per month from people who think they are going to break their motherboard if they push any harder. The answer every time is push harder. Just make sure you have your RAM properly aligned with the DIMM slot.

Do the Cable Management as You Go

Not much to say here. Always do your cable management as you go. It will save you time and a lot of effort later, that is, if you even get to it later.
